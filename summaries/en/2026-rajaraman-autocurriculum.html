Provable Benefits of Autocurriculum

A learning-theory account of why letting a reasoning model pick which prompts to train on — using nothing but a verifier's pass/fail signal — cuts the cost of both SFT and RL, sometimes exponentially.

SECTION 01

Problem — reasoning is expensive, can we make it cheaper?

Chain-of-thought reasoning models are trained two ways, and both are costly. This paper asks whether those costs are fundamental, or an artifact of not being adaptive about which problems you train on.

Modern reasoning models (o1, R1, Phi-reasoning, …) get their power from chain-of-thought (CoT): spending extra tokens before answering. Producing them relies on two expensive pipelines:

SFT / Distillation

Collect long, high-quality reasoning traces from a strong teacher (human or frontier model) and fit the student on them. Statistically expensive — every teacher demonstration is precious.

RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)

No teacher traces, but you must roll out a huge number of reasoning attempts from the model under training and score them with a verifier. Computationally expensive — generation dominates the bill.

The standard recipe treats every prompt equally: query the teacher on all prompts, or roll out on all prompts. But many prompts are already easy for the current model — spending expensive supervision on them is waste. The central question:

How should a reasoning model design its own curriculum, and what are the statistical & computational benefits of doing so?

The paper's answer: autocurriculum — using a cheap outcome verifier (a unit test, an answer checker) to decide which prompts the model currently struggles on, and focusing expensive supervision there. Crucially, this needs no assumptions on prompt distribution or difficulty; the curriculum emerges purely from the model's own training dynamics. The machinery is classical: boosting (Freund & Schapire, 1997) and learning from counterexamples (Angluin, 1987).

SECTION 02

Setup — models, verifiers, and two learning settings

Everything is phrased in a clean autoregressive learning-theory framework, so that "cost" has a precise meaning.

Language models & chain-of-thought

A prompt space X ⊆ Σ* carries a target distribution ρ. A model π : Σ* → ΔΣ is a next-token predictor, shared across positions (parameter-sharing regime). Autoregressive decoding for T steps induces the CoT distribution π1:T(·|x) over length-T sequences; the last token is the "answer".

Accuracy via an outcome verifier

An outcome verifier V : X × Σ → {0,1} returns 1 iff a final answer is correct for a prompt — think unit test or math answer checker. Model accuracy is:

Accρ(π) = Ex∼ρ Ey∼π1:T(·|x) [ V(x, yT) ]
avg over prompts of the probability the final token is a correct answer

The learner works in the realizable setting (Assumption 2.1): the model class Π contains a π* with Accρ(π*) = 1. The complexity of Π is its Natarajan dimension d = Ndim(Π) ≤ log₂|Π|.

SFT setting

A CoT oracle CoT(x) returns a perfect teacher trace y ∼ π*1:T(·|x). Cost = nCoT, the number of teacher traces queried. This is what you want to minimize (traces are expensive).

RLVR setting

No teacher. Instead a reference model πref you can sample. Cost = ncomp, the total number of length-T rollouts generated during training (from πref or any learner model).

In both settings the learner also has free query access to the verifier V. That cheap pass/fail signal — separate from the expensive supervision — is what makes autocurriculum possible.

SFT — the 1/ε vanishes from CoT cost

Θ̃(d/ε) → Õ(d)

The number of teacher demonstrations drops from scaling with target accuracy to being nearly independent of it. An exponential improvement in 1/ε. You still see many prompts (cheap), but only ask the teacher on Õ(d) of them.

RLVR — coverage decouples from accuracy

Õ(dCseq/ε) → Õ(dCseq + d/ε)

The coverage cost Cseq becomes a one-time burn-in that no longer multiplies 1/ε. Beyond burn-in, improving accuracy costs as if coverage were perfect.

The one-sentence intuition: the verifier is a cheap oracle that tells you where you're wrong; boosting turns "fix a constant fraction of your current mistakes" into geometric convergence, so you only need O(log(1/ε)) rounds of expensive supervision instead of O(1/ε) worth.

SECTION 04

AutoTune — boosting your way through SFT

The core algorithm. It's classical boosting-by-filtering, with the outcome verifier playing the role of the reweighting oracle.

The baseline it beats

Prior work (Joshi et al., 2025; Foster et al., 2024) collects a CoT on every prompt and does next-token prediction (NTP). For deterministic classes under 0-1 loss this needs (their Prop. 3.1):

nprompt = nCoT ≲ [ d·log(dT|Σ|)·log(1/ε) + log(1/δ) ] / ε  , d = Ndim(Π)

The 1/ε out front is the enemy: to reach 99.9% accuracy you pay for a thousand times more teacher traces than to reach 90%. Autocurriculum kills it.

The idea: a verifier-guided ensemble of weak learners

AutoTune (Algorithm 1) runs k = O(log(1/ε)) phases. In each phase it trains a new model to merely constant accuracy (a weak learner, error err★ = ¼) — but on a reweighted prompt distribution that up-weights where the current ensemble errs. The verifier decides which prompts count as errors; the teacher is queried only on those. The final model f̂ is improper: a plurality vote over the ensemble.

Split & loop over phases

Split the prompt dataset into k parts; run phase j = 0 … k−1, growing an ensemble Πj.

Sample where the ensemble is wrong (Algorithm 2)

A rejection-sampling subroutine Sample(·) reweights prompts toward those the current ensemble mislabels — using only verifier calls. Weight wj(x) = αj,krankj(x), where rankj(x) counts how many ensemble members already answer x correctly; the boosting weights α come from Freund's boosting-by-filtering.

Train a weak learner on the hard slice

Query the teacher for CoTs only on the sampled (hard) prompts and fit a new model π̂j to constant accuracy via the base NTP learner.

Aggregate by plurality vote

Return Plu({π̂ : π̂ ∈ Πk}) — the outcome-level model that takes the ensemble's most-voted answer.

Why boosting works here (Fig 2 intuition): each new weak learner is trained to fix a constant fraction of the ensemble's remaining errors on a larger measure of prompts than it breaks. So error shrinks geometrically — the ensemble's accuracy approaches 1 at rate ~(3/4)k, hence only k = O(log(1/ε)) rounds are needed.

Fig 2 · Each weak learner corrects a plurality region of prior errors (✓) while introducing few new ones (✗); under the right reweighting, ensemble accuracy climbs toward 1 geometrically (redrawn from paper Figure 2).

Main result — Theorem 3.3 (exponential improvement)

For deterministic Π, a sparse verifier (Assumption 3.1: each prompt has a unique correct answer), and realizable π*: if the prompt dataset has size n ≥ (d/ε)·polylog(ε⁻¹, δ⁻¹, T, |Σ|), then AutoTune returns an outcome-level f̂ with Accρ(f̂) ≥ 1−ε w.p. 1−δ, and the number of teacher CoT queries is only

nCoT ≤ d · polylog(ε⁻¹, δ⁻¹, T, |Σ|)
— nearly independent of the target accuracy 1−ε

Not just active learning. Classical active learning also gets log(1/ε) label complexity — but only under structural assumptions (bounded disagreement coefficient / star number). AutoTune needs none of those, on any Π. The trick: the learner extracts information about π* by querying the verifier, a cheaper feedback channel that sidesteps active-learning lower bounds. Problem 3.2 is genuinely not a special case of active learning.

The outcome-level caveat (Remark 3.5): the Õ(d) CoT bound only holds when accuracy is measured on the final answer. If you demand correctness on the entire CoT sequence, it degrades back to Ω̃(d/ε) — the verifier gives no signal about intermediate tokens.

SECTION 05

General (stochastic) models — the realistic case

Real language models aren't deterministic. Section 3.4 extends the result to stochastic Π, at the price of relaxing "high accuracy" to "moderate accuracy on most prompts."

AutoTunestoch (Algorithm 3) replaces the 0-1 loss with a thresholded loss ℓx(π) = 𝟙[Accx(π) ≥ 4/5] and uses smaller weak-learner error (err★ = 1/400). Theorem 3.6: with n ≥ (log|Π|/ε)·polylog prompts, it queries the CoT oracle at most

nCoT ≤ log|Π| · polylog(ε⁻¹, δ⁻¹, T)
and returns f̂ with   Prx∼ρ[ Accx(f̂) ≥ 3/5 ] ≥ 1−ε

This is a bound on the model's pass@k rate (Chen, 2021): on a 1−ε mass of prompts, a decent fraction of independent attempts succeed. It's implementable with O(log(1/ε)) calls to a log-loss ERM oracle for Π.

Sharpening to high accuracy (Remark 3.7). If answers are unique, consensus voting upgrades the guarantee: sample N = Ω(log(1/ε)) answers from f̂ and return the plurality. The resulting f̂cons satisfies Accρ(f̂cons) ≥ 1 − O(ε) — moderate-accuracy pass@k is sharpened into high accuracy at inference time.

SECTION 06

RLVR — decoupling coverage from accuracy

The second setting: no teacher, only a pretrained reference model. Here autocurriculum reduces compute rather than teacher labels.

Background — what is RLVR?

RLVR (Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards) is the dominant post-training recipe for reasoning models. Instead of a learned reward model (as in RLHF), the reward comes from a deterministic verifier that checks whether an output is objectively correct: run the unit tests, or compare the final answer to the known solution. The model generates attempts (rollouts), each gets a 0/1 verifiable reward, and a policy-gradient method (PPO, GRPO, …) pushes it toward higher-reward behavior. This is exactly the πref + outcome-verifier V setup formalized in this paper.

Origin

The idea of RL against auto-verifiable outcomes traces to DeepSeekMath (Shao et al., 2024, which also introduced GRPO). The term "RLVR" was coined & popularized by AI2's Tülu 3 (Lambert et al., 2024) — the paper's citation for RLVR. DeepSeek-R1 (2025) then made it famous.

Why it's popular

No reward-model training (cheaper, less bias); hard to reward-hack since the signal is ground truth; scales automatically with no humans in the loop; and it targets exactly the verifiable domains — math & code — where CoT reasoning pays off.

The catch

Its cost is compute: on hard prompts you must roll out many attempts just to find a single correct one. That is precisely the ncomp ∼ Õ(dCseq/ε) cost this paper attacks with an autocurriculum.

RLVR primer sources · Tülu 3 (Lambert et al., 2024) · DeepSeekMath (Shao et al., 2024) · Ai2 Tülu 3 blog · RLVR overview (AI Infra KB) · RLVR (Label Studio)

Coverage — the RL analogue of realizability

You start from a reference πref and must guide it to high accuracy using only the verifier. The key quantity is sequence-level coverage (Definition 4.2): πref covers with parameter Cseq if it places at least Cseq−1 probability on the correct CoT for every prompt.

πref(y*x | x) ≥ Cseq−1  for every prompt x  (smaller Cseq = better coverage)

Baseline — filtered SFT via rejection sampling

RLFineTune (Algorithm 4): for each prompt draw m = O(Cseq) candidate CoTs from πref, keep only those the verifier accepts (and that have decent probability), then fit a model to the survivors. Proposition 4.3 shows this is tractable, but its compute scales as

ncomp ∼ Õ( d · Cseq / ε )
— coverage cost Cseq multiplies the accuracy cost 1/ε. Painful.

You must generate ~Õ(Cseq) traces per prompt just to see a single correct CoT, and you pay that for every level of accuracy.

Fig 3 · Autocurriculum for RL: the verifier up-weights failed prompts in each batch; the model is improved with an RL update on those (redrawn from paper Figure 3).

Main result — Theorem 4.5 (autocurriculum decouples coverage from accuracy)

AutoTune.RL (Algorithm 5) wraps the same boosting outer loop around RLFineTune as the base learner. With n ≥ (d/ε)·polylog(Cseq, ε⁻¹, δ⁻¹, T), it returns f̂ with Accρ(f̂) ≥ 1−ε, and (up to polylog):

ncomp ≤ Õ( d·Cseq + d/ε ) vs. baseline Õ(d·Cseq/ε)
verifier calls ≤ Õ( d·Cseq + d/ε )

Why it works. Each weak learner trains on only Õ(d) prompts, so the per-prompt generation cost Õ(Cseq) no longer multiplies 1/ε — it becomes a one-time burn-in Õ(dCseq) that grows only logarithmically with 1/ε. The remaining Õ(d/ε) — routing prompts through the ensemble — is nearly independent of coverage and matches the cost of a reference model with perfect coverage. Beyond the burn-in you pay as if coverage were O(1).

These aren't new heuristics. The paper positions well-known systems as instances of its theory: DAPO's filtering of all-correct/all-incorrect rollouts and PCL's value-model prompt selection are heuristic versions of the verifier-guided filtering in AutoTune; ReST and the distillation stage of DeepSeek-R1 are essentially the rejection-sampling of RLFineTune. The contribution is quantifying when wrapping these in an outer autocurriculum provably reduces total compute.

SECTION 07

Limitations & open directions

The framework buys its clean bounds with simplifying assumptions; the authors are explicit about which, and Part II is teased for the biggest one.

Fixed reference / no self-play

The RLVR results assume a fixed πref with given coverage Cseq. Practical methods (ReST, expert iteration) use the current model as reference, improving coverage over rounds. Whether the Õ(dCseq) burn-in shrinks under self-play is open.

Batch, not online

Algorithms are batch: train on a fixed set, re-evaluate. Modern pipelines use online policy gradients (PPO, GRPO) where the model updates continuously. Extending autocurriculum to co-evolving prompt distribution + model is future work.

Beyond coverage — the big one

RLVR requires πref to cover the optimal CoT on every prompt. When coverage fails (prompts far beyond current ability), filtering just gives up. Whether autocurriculum enables coverage expansion — training on easy problems to build coverage on hard ones — is deferred to the forthcoming Part II.

Perfect verification

The theory assumes a perfect outcome verifier — natural for math/code, but extending to noisy or learned reward models is an important open problem.

Bottom line. The paper's message is a clean separation of concerns: a cheap verifier tells you where you're wrong; boosting turns that into geometric convergence; so expensive supervision (teacher CoTs, or coverage-limited rollouts) is spent O(log(1/ε)) times instead of O(1/ε) times. The improvements are purely from adaptive data selection — no assumption on prompt distribution or difficulty required.
